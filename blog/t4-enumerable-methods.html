#Group_by

Lannisters vs. Everyone Else

15 January 2015

After a brief introduction to Ruby and some of its methods last week, I've begun to dig deeper into the language this week. Specifically, I've focused on looking at the Enumerable module, which contains a large variety of versatile methods that can be used on many objects, including collections such as arrays and hashes. In this post I'll be hashing out the method #group_by (which produces hashes! But more on that in a second...).

#Group_by is powerful method that takes an array as its input and returns a hash. The elements of the array become the values of the hash, and the block specifies what the keys of the hash will be. In layman's terms, this means that a list of objects goes in, and those objects come out grouped together based on a similar characteristic. (You can refer to the Ruby docs explanation of #group_by here.) This may sound a bit confusing, so let's elucidate #group_by with an example.

Let's say you have a list of names.

names = ["Tyrion Lannister", "Jaime Lannister", "Arya Stark", 
"Jon Snow", "Tywin Lannister", "Eddard Stark", 
"Robb Stark", "Sansa Stark", "Cersei Lannister", 
"Joffrey 'Baratheon' (but we all know he's really a Lannister)", 
"Daenerys Targaryen", "Brienne Tarth", "Bran Stark"]
You want to separate the list of names into two groups: Lannisters and everyone else.
names.group_by {|name| name.include?("Lannister")}
# => {true=>["Tyrion Lannister", "Jaime Lannister", "Tywin Lannister", 
# "Cersei Lannister", "Joffrey 'Baratheon' (but we all know 
# he's really a Lannister)"], 
# => false=>["Arya Stark", "Jon Snow", "Eddard Stark", "Robb Stark", 
# "Sansa Stark", "Daenerys Targaryen", "Brienne Tarth", "Bran Stark"]}
And there you are! Now you have two groups: a group of Lannisters, and a group of everyone else (since the Lannisters only care about their own, and often not even that).

You may be wondering why the returned hash has "true" and "false" keys. This is because the method called within the block (#include?) asked for a boolean, or a true or false value. A helpful hint: any time you see a method with a '?' at the end of it, it's going to return a boolean!

As you may be able to guess, the #group_by method can be very useful, depending upon your task. It works beautifully in ordering a list of lists by size, a list of strings by length, or even a list of integers by the remainder when dividing by another integer. To learn more about useful ways to implement the #group_by method, take a look at this blog post and also this one. Happy coding!
